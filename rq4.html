Average Age Group Offences by Jurisdiction

Identifying how the most recent average age group of speeding offences vary across jurisdictions.
Data Period: 2024 only

Youth Is The Dominant Risk

Drivers aged 17–25 are the highest-risk group: they offend at roughly 9× the rate of seniors (65+), a universal pattern across every jurisdiction.

Volume Concentration: QLD & VIC

Queensland and Victoria together account for about 40% of all infractions—largely reflecting their population size but also indicating consistent enforcement coverage.

Per-Capita Patterns

License holder counts scale closely with infractions. NSW holds ~40% of licensees and ~35% of infractions, suggesting enforcement is broadly proportional to population.

Age Group Analysis

Youth Risk Dominance

Young drivers (17–25) are the single largest risk group by rate. Even after adjusting for license-holder counts, this cohort shows the highest offence rates in every state.

  • Relative risk: ~9× higher than drivers aged 65+
  • Policy implication: targeted education, graduated licensing reinforcement, and young-driver enforcement remain high-impact levers.

Age-Related Decline

There is a smooth, consistent decline in offending as drivers age. The pattern is stable across jurisdictions and suggests experience reduces risky driving.

  • Age distribution (share): 17–25: 21.8%, 26–39: 15.6%, 40–64: 9.6%, 65+: 2.5%
  • Interpretation: steady decline in risk — driver maturity and experience appear protective.

State Volume Differences

Total infractions vary substantially by state, reflecting population differences and enforcement scale. NSW, QLD and VIC account for the majority of offences.

  • Approximate totals (from dataset): NSW ≈ 185,000, QLD ≈ 780,000, VIC ≈ 760,000
  • QLD & VIC together ≈ 40% of national infractions.

Middle-Aged Drivers: Safest Per Capita

The 40–64 age group, while large in absolute numbers, has the lowest offence rate per license holder — highlighting driver maturity and experience as protective factors.

  • 40–64 group offence share: 9.6% (lowest per-capita rate)
  • Implication: interventions yield most benefit when targeted at younger cohorts rather than broadly across all ages.
